Design studios establish project requirements, define the problem, and explore many solutions. When the Open Data, Design, and Development (ODDD) team took on this complex project of adding federal sales data to the [revenuedata.doi.gov](revenuedata.doi.gov) site (aka NRRD), we decided that this assignment required design studios. Design studios would make sure we developed the best possible design and content solutions on a short timeline.

In the past, we limited design studio participants to ODDD team members. For this project, we included subject matter experts (SMEs) from various teams throughout the Office of Natural Resources Revenue (ONRR) for 2 reasons: 
- Our team wants to increase user and stakeholder participation in our design work.   
- SMEs had the in-depth content knowledge we needed, as well as ideas of how customers might use this data.

Some of our SMEs use this federal sales dataset and NRRD, so we were able to incorporate some users into our design studios. Due to project restrictions, we were not able to include external users in the design studios. Now that it is publicly available, we hope to include external users in future studies related to this dataset.
After the design studios, we performed user research on the prototypes and webpages within our development site. This allowed us to gather user feedback and make impactful updates.

## Background
This project started with requirements from outside of our agency. ONRR received a [recommendation](https://www.oversight.gov/sites/default/files/oig-reports/DOI/Final-Inspection-ReportEffectiveRoyaltyRatesPublic.pdf) from the Office of the Inspector General (OIG) to “Develop and implement a means of communicating the oil and gas effective royalty rates to stakeholders and decision makers on an ongoing basis.” Given the short project timeline, we were not able to perform discovery research. Discovery research would help us determine the demand for effective royalty rate data on our public sites. Participants in previous user research studies requested federal sales data on NRRD. This federal sales dataset is a way that we can include the effective royalty rate. Publishing this dataset and effective royalty rate would meet the OIG recommendation.

## Design Studios
For the [design studios](https://blog-nrrd.doi.gov/trends-design-studio/) we adapted our process to meet the needs and requirements of the OIG recommendation. We typically start design studios with a session where we “define appetite”. Appetite is our interest and capacity to take on the project. Because the OIG recommendation required us to publish federal sales data, defining appetite was not an effective use of our time. We already knew we had to complete the project and had a deadline.

Instead, we started with recruiting SMEs. These SMEs were designated by the ONRR team that owns the dataset. Our design studio group consisted of 7 SMEs from 3 different ONRR teams and our ODDD team of 6.

Over the course of 5 sessions, we established requirements, created and reviewed low-fidelity prototypes, hole-poked, and refined high-fidelity prototypes. Our two ODDD designers facilitated the sessions and organized offline work. We emphasized building comfort and confidence with the SMEs. We went into the studios with the mindset that everyone is a designer, but we acknowledged that people who weren’t formally trained in design find it difficult to see themselves as designers. To help them see themselves as designers, we were very flexible on what constituted a prototype. We used different facilitation methods that ensured all participants had equal space to give ideas and feedback. We also asked them to send us feedback outside of the sessions, which allowed the SMEs to process the information and give feedback at their own pace.

Here’s a breakdown of what took place during and between each design studio session:

### Studio 1
We introduced the SMEs to the design studio process and expectations. Each participant shared their backgrounds, experiences with the dataset, and project concerns. This sharing helped participants build understanding and empathy for each other. We compiled an agreed upon list of requirements. As a group we toured NRRD and the various sections where sales data could be added. We also explained the low-fidelity (lo-fi) prototyping assignment and provided examples.

**Offline work:**
- Participants created lo-fi prototypes and drafts for charts, graphs, text content, and process diagrams.
- Participants produced prototypes in any way they were most comfortable. This included pencil sketches, PowerPoint charts, spreadsheets, and annotated screenshots.

### Studio 2
We shared the lo-fi prototypes to the group. Each participant presented their prototype and other participants asked questions.

**Offline work:**
ODDD facilitators used a survey to gather feedback on the prototypes. In a typical design studio, the feedback is usually given in real time. Since most of this group was new to design studios, we changed this studio’s format to fit the participants’ needs for more anonymous feedback. Participants gave feedback on the prototypes via survey. Only the designer knew who submitted each survey so that they could ask follow-up questions as needed.

### Studio 3
We reviewed the results from our feedback survey and discussed the top ideas for each section using the requirements list from Studio 1. We also discussed possible solutions that were realistic with our resources and timeline.  This is also known as “hole-poking”. The group agreed on which ideas and lo-fi prototypes would be developed into high-fidelity (hi-fi) prototypes.

**Offline work:**
Designers created interactive, functional hi-fi prototypes. The two designers on the project divvied up the various parts of the site for prototyping so each designer was working on different pages.

### Studio 4
We split Studio 4 into two sessions to allow plenty of time for presenting and getting input on the hi-fi prototypes. Splitting Studio 4 would also prevent burnout that might come with an extra-long single session. The group continued hole-poking with these hi-fi prototypes.

**Offline work:**
- Designers continued to refine prototypes. Any additional feedback on the prototypes was gathered via surveys and email. Feedback was also gathered from key stakeholders in ONRR who were not involved in the design studios. Using additional stakeholders, helped ensure the requirements from leadership were being met. It also kept them informed and engaged with the work that went into the project.
- Sessions 4a and 4b completed the design studio. Our team updated participants with details of the hi-fi prototypes' refinements, pages developed, and user research completed on the [query](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/query-data/?dataType=Federal%20Sales) and [download data](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/downloads/federal-sales/) pages for this dataset.

## User testing
The design studios provided the team with a space to identify requirements, explore options through prototyping, and converge in agreement around some solutions. User testing allowed us to further explore the usability of the prototypes. User testing helped us find areas of improvement and incorporate user feedback into the final product.

For user testing, we recruited participants outside of the design studio team to interview. We gathered opinions and insights from participants who were unaware of the OIG recommendation. These participants were also unfamiliar with internal processes related to this data. Our leadership directed us to recruit participants only from U.S. Department of Interior (DOI) employees. By recruiting DOI participants (external to ONRR), we gathered feedback from people who may use ONRR data but are unfamiliar with ONRR’s internal processes.

**Round 1** of user testing included usability testing for prototypes of the download data and query pages. The ODDD team held a total of 13 user interviews with each lasting about 45 minutes. The participants provided valuable information, including 43 insights and 32 recommendations. Of the 32 recommendations, ODDD implemented 26 immediately to better align the product design with user needs.

**Round 2** of user testing included hallway testing for the explore data page. Three ONRR employees participated in sessions that were approximately 10 minutes long. The hallway testing resulted in 4 recommendations for the explore data page. Because this page is complex, the ODDD team decided to wait to publish the federal sales data on the explore data page. More user testing is required before the page is developed.

## Publishing the federal sales data

Through the design studios and user testing, our team published federal sales data 7 weeks before the deadline set by ONRR leadership. This initial roll-out included:
- the [query tool](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/query-data),
- the [download data page](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/downloads/federal-sales/), and
- a redesign of the [homepage](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/) “what’s new” box to promote this newly added dataset.

## Next steps for federal sales data
- Build explore data page for federal sales data.
- Perform usability testing on explore data using a beta site, incorporate recommendations, and publish the page.
- Perform user research with external stakeholders and incorporate recommendations.

## Lessons learned
The team held a meeting to identify the lessons we learned from this project to improve our design practice next time. This project’s lessons learned include:

- **Identify roles on the ODDD team** for the design studio and user testing parts of this project. This includes roles, such as a project manager, interview, recruitment, and analysis.
- **Communicate the larger project plan** to the team to help teammates consider the level of effort required for the project in general. This includes defining each task, the timeline of each task, prioritization of the project in comparison to other work, and general expectations.
- **Provide basic notes on decisions made and action items** to send out to team and participants in design studios. This could be in a running list of notes that is sent out before or after each meeting. This will help participants stay on the same page about decisions made and action items they are responsible for.
- Continue to plan projects that **move the team towards participatory design**. This project was the first time the team included people outside of ODDD in design studios. These additional stakeholders enhanced the team’s understanding of this complex dataset and the end user.
