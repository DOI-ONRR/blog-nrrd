Design studios establish project requirements, define the problem, and explore many solutions. When the Open Data, Design, and Discovery (ODDD) team was tasked with the complex project of adding federal sales data to the [revenuedata.doi.gov](revenuedata.doi.gov) site (aka NRRD), we decided that this assignment required design studios to make sure we developed the best possible design and content solutions on a short timeline.

In the past, we limited design studio participants to ODDD team members. For this project we included subject matter experts (SMEs) from various teams throughout the Office of Natural Resources Revenue (ONRR) for 2 reasons: 
- We’ve been looking for ways to increase user and stakeholder participation in our design work     
- SMEs had the in-depth content knowledge we needed, as well as ideas of how customers might use this data. 

Some of our ONRR SMEs use this federal sales dataset and NRRD, so we were able to incorporate some users into our design studios. Due to project restrictions, we were not able to include external users      in the design studios. Now that it is publicly available, we hope to include external users in future studies related to this dataset.
Following the design studios for this project, we performed user research on the prototypes and webpages within our development site so that we could gather user feedback.

## Background
This project started with requirements from outside of our agency. ONRR received a [recommendation](https://www.oversight.gov/sites/default/files/oig-reports/DOI/Final-Inspection-ReportEffectiveRoyaltyRatesPublic.pdf) from the Office of the Inspector General (OIG) to “Develop and implement a means of communicating the oil and gas effective royalty rates to stakeholders and decision makers on an ongoing basis.” Given the relatively short project timeline, we were not able to perform discovery research to determine the demand for effective royalty rate data on our public sites. However, based on comments from participants in previous user research studies, we knew there was a desire for sales data on NRRD, and sales data is a means by which to communicate the effective royalty rate. Publishing the federal sales dataset and accompanying information would meet the recommendation from OIG.

## Design Studios
For this [design studios](https://blog-nrrd.doi.gov/trends-design-studio/) we adapted our process to meet the needs and requirements of the OIG recommendation. We typically start design studios with a session where we “define appetite” – discussing as a group our interest and capacity to take on the project. Since adding federal sales data to the site was necessary to meet the OIG recommendation, a session to determine appetite was not an effective use of our time. We already knew we had to complete the project and had a deadline.

Instead, we started with recruiting SMEs. These SMEs were designated by the ONRR team that owns the dataset. Our design studio group consisted of 7 SMEs from 3 different ONRR teams and our ODDD team of 6.

Over the course of 5 sessions, we established requirements, created and reviewed low-fidelity prototypes, hole-poked, and refined high-fidelity prototypes. Our two ODDD designers facilitated the sessions and organized offline work. We emphasized building comfort and confidence with the SMEs. Although we went into the studios with the mindset that everyone is a designer, we’ve learned from this project and others that non-”Designers” (or those who weren’t formally trained in design) often find it difficult to see themselves as designers. To help them see themselves as designers, we were very flexible on what constituted a prototype, and used facilitation methods that ensured all participants had equal space to give ideas and feedback. We also asked them to send us feedback outside of the sessions, which allowed the SMEs to process the information and give feedback at their own pace.

Here’s a breakdown of what took place during and between each design studio session:

### Studio 1
We introduced the SMEs to the design studio process and expectations. By sharing participants’ backgrounds, experiences with the dataset, and project concerns, participants developed an understanding and built empathy within the group. We compiled an agreed upon list of requirements. As a group we toured NRRD and the various sections where sales data will be added. We also explained the low-fidelity (lo-fi) prototyping assignment and provided examples since most of the group had never participated in a design process.

*Offline work:*
- Participants created low-fidelity prototypes and drafts for charts, graphs, text content, and process diagrams. 
- Participants produced prototypes in any way they were most comfortable with - including pencil sketches, PowerPoint charts, spreadsheets, and annotated screenshots from the existing sites.

### Studio 2
We shared the lo-fi prototypes and each participant was given time to present their prototype and other participants can ask questions.

*Offline work:*
ODDD facilitators used a survey to gather feedback on the prototypes. In a typical design studio, the feedback is usually given in real time. Since most of this group was new to design studios, we changed this studio’s format to fit the participants’ desire to give more anonymous feedback. For the survey, only the designer knew who commented on the different prototypes so they could ask follow-up questions as needed.

### Studio 3
We reviewed the results from our feedback survey and discussed the top ideas for each section in terms of the requirements list from session 1. We also discussed possible solutions that were realistic with our resources and timeline.  This is also known as “hole-poking”. The group agreed on which ideas and lo-fi prototypes would be developed into high-fidelity (hi-fi) prototypes.

*Offline work:*
Designers created interactive, functional hi-fi prototypes. With two designers on the project, the various parts of the site were divvied up so each designer was working on different pages. 

### Studio 4
We split Studio 4 into 2 sessions to allow plenty of time for presenting and getting input on the hi-fi prototypes, and to prevent burnout that might come with an extra-long single session. The group continued hole-poking with these hi-fi prototypes. 

*Offline work:*
- Designers continued to refine prototypes. Any additional feedback on the prototypes was gathered via surveys and email. Feedback was also gathered from key stakeholders in ONRR who were not involved in the sessions. This helped ensure the requirements from leadership were being met, and also kept them informed and engaged with the work that went into the project.
- Sessions 4a and 4b completed the design studio. Participants were kept updated as the hi-fi prototypes were refined, pages developed, and user research completed on the [query](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/query-data/?dataType=Federal%20Sales) and [download data](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/downloads/federal-sales/) pages for this dataset.

## User testing
The design studios provided the team with a space to identify requirements, explore options through prototyping, and converge in agreement around some solutions for the federal sales data. User testing is the part of the ODDD team’s process that allows us to further explore the usability of the prototypes. User testing helps us find areas of improvement and incorporate user feedback into the final product.

For user testing, we recruited participants outside of the design studio team to interview. This allowed us to get opinions and insights from participants that were less familiar with the OIG recommendation and data-related internal processes. With direction provided by leadership, participants for user testing only included U.S. Department of Interior (DOI) employees. We recruited participants employed within DOI (but external to ONRR) to gain the perspective of a stakeholder who may use ONRR data but is not familiar with ONRR’s internal processes.

*Round 1* of user testing included usability testing for prototypes of the download data and query pages. The ODDD team held a total of 13 user interviews with each lasting about 45 minutes. The participants provided our team with valuable information, including 43 insights and 32 recommendations. Of the 32 recommendations, 26 were implemented immediately to better align the product design with user needs. 

*Round 2* of user testing was done in the form of hallway testing for the explore data page. Three ONRR employees participated in sessions that were approximately 10 minutes long. The hallway testing resulted in 4 recommendations for the explore data page. Because this page is very complex, the ODDD team decided to wait to publish the federal sales data on the explore data page until more user testing could be completed.

## Publishing the federal sales data
Through the design studios and user testing, our team published federal sales data 7 weeks before the deadline set by ONRR leadership. This initial roll-out included: 
- the [query tool](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/query-data), 
- the [download data page](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/downloads/federal-sales/), and 
- a redesign of the [homepage](https://revenuedata.doi.gov/) “what’s new” box to promote this newly added dataset.
## Next steps for federal sales data
- Build explore data page for federal sales data.
- Perform usability testing on explore data using a beta site, incorporate recommendations, and publish the page.
- Perform user research with external stakeholders and incorporate recommendations.

## Lessons learned
The team held a meeting to identify the lessons we learned from this project to improve our design practice next time. This project’s lessons learned include:
- Identify roles on the ODDD team for the design studio and user testing parts of this project, such as a project manager, interview, recruitment, and analysis. 
- Communicate the larger project plan to the team to help teammates consider the level of effort required for the project in general. This includes defining each task, the timeline of each task, prioritization of the project in comparison to other work, and general expectations. 
- Provide basic notes on decisions made and action items to send out to team and participants in design studios. This could be in a running list of notes that is sent out before or after each meeting. This will help participants stay on the same page about decisions made and action items they are responsible for.
- Continue to plan projects that move the team towards participatory design. This project was the first time the team included people outside of ODDD in design studios, which enhanced the team’s understanding of this complex dataset and the end user.
